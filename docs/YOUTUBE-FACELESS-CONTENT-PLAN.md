# YouTube Faceless Content Plan — SPD Cert Prep

## Format Overview

**"Faceless" means:** No camera, no face, no voice required if you don't want it.
All videos use screen recordings, stock footage, text animations, and either AI voiceover or your own voice recorded separately.

**Production stack (free/cheap):**
- **Screen recording:** OBS Studio (free)
- **Editing:** CapCut (free, good for text animations) or DaVinci Resolve (free, pro-level)
- **AI voiceover:** ElevenLabs (~$5/month) or TikTok TTS style via CapCut
- **Background music:** YouTube Audio Library (free, no copyright)
- **Thumbnails:** Canva (free tier)
- **Stock footage:** Pexels.com (free), Pixabay (free)

### Series B: "SPD Standards Explained" (Pillar 3 — SEO)

Short, specific videos that rank for technical search terms.

#### VIDEO B1: "AAMI ST79 Explained in 5 Minutes"

**Format:** Animated text slides + voiceover
**Length:** 4–6 minutes
**Target keyword:** `AAMI ST79 sterile processing`

**Script:**

---

[INTRO — 0:00–0:20]

"AAMI ST79 is the most important standard in sterile processing. If you're studying for the CRCST exam — or just trying to understand why your department does what it does — this video breaks it down in plain English."

[WHAT IS IT — 0:20–1:00]

"AAMI stands for the Association for the Advancement of Medical Instrumentation. ST79 is their comprehensive guide to steam sterilization and sterility assurance in healthcare facilities.

It's not a law — but it's adopted by The Joint Commission and CMS as the standard of practice. So if your facility isn't following ST79, it's at risk of citations and patient safety issues."

[KEY SECTIONS — 1:00–4:00]

"ST79 covers five major areas:

One — Cleaning and decontamination. Instruments must be cleaned before sterilization. Biofilm and organic debris can block sterilant penetration and cause sterilization failure.

Two — Inspection and assembly. Every instrument should be inspected for functionality, cleanliness, and damage before packaging. Hinged instruments should be opened. Lumened instruments need proper air removal.

Three — Packaging. The packaging must allow sterilant penetration and maintain sterility until point of use. Damaged packaging means the item is no longer sterile, regardless of what the indicators say.

Four — Sterilization cycle parameters. Temperature, time, and pressure requirements vary by sterilizer type. Know your gravity vs. pre-vacuum cycles, and your minimum exposure times.

Five — Sterility assurance. This includes biological indicators, chemical indicators, and physical monitoring. All three should be used together — no single method is sufficient alone."

[EXAM RELEVANCE — 4:00–4:30]

"On the CRCST exam, AAMI ST79 is referenced constantly. Packaging integrity, BI incubation, and cycle monitoring questions almost always trace back to this standard.

If you want to practice applying ST79 concepts in exam-format questions, SPD Cert Prep has a full section covering AAMI standards. Link in the description."

#### VIDEO B2: "Spaulding Classification — The Foundation of Disinfection Decisions"

**Format:** Animated diagram + voiceover
**Length:** 4–5 minutes
**Target keyword:** `Spaulding classification sterile processing`

**Script:**

---

[INTRO]

"Every disinfection decision in a hospital traces back to one framework developed in 1968 — the Spaulding Classification. If you understand this, disinfection questions on the CRCST exam become much easier."

[THE THREE LEVELS]

"Dr. Earle Spaulding divided medical devices into three categories based on their risk of infection transmission.

Category one: Critical items. These contact sterile tissue or the vascular system — surgical instruments, implants, cardiac catheters. They must be sterilized. No exceptions. Any contamination here can cause a life-threatening infection.

Category two: Semi-critical items. These contact mucous membranes or non-intact skin — endoscopes, laryngoscopes, respiratory therapy equipment. They require high-level disinfection at minimum. Sterilization is preferred when feasible.

Category three: Non-critical items. These contact only intact skin — blood pressure cuffs, stethoscopes, bed rails. Low-level or intermediate-level disinfection is appropriate."

[COMMON EXAM TRAPS]

"Where exam questions try to trick you: flexible endoscopes. They're semi-critical, so they require high-level disinfection. But if they're used in a sterile body cavity — like during ERCP — they should be sterilized.

Another trap: the category applies to the item's intended use, not the item itself. A flexible scope that only contacts mucous membranes is semi-critical. The same scope touching sterile tissue becomes critical."

## Thumbnail Templates

All thumbnails follow this pattern for brand consistency:

- **Background:** Dark navy (#0A1628) or dark teal (#0D3B38)
- **Main text:** 3–5 words max, bold white or amber (#F59E0B)
- **Accent:** Single graphic element (checkmark, ❌, question mark, dollar sign)
- **No faces needed** — icons and bold text outperform stock photos for this niche

- "24 DOMAINS" in huge bold type → "CRCST EXAM BREAKDOWN" smaller below
